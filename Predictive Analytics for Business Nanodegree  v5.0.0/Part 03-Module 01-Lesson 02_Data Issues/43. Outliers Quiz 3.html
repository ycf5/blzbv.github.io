q3

Adjusting for outliers

Update the data in the Outliers Quiz file using the following steps.

  • Update the outlier in row 15 such that the age is 56
  • Create 3 new worksheet tabs, each with a copy of the data. We assume you're using a spreadsheet software such as Excel.
  • Rename the tabs Outlier1, Outlier2, Outlier3
  • In the Outlier1 tab, delete rows 14 and 15 (removing outliers 2 & 3 from the dataset)
  • In the Outlier2 tab, delete rows 10 and 15 (removing outliers 1 & 3 from the dataset)
  • In the Outlier3 tab, delete rows 10 and 14 (removing outliers 1 & 2)
  • Create a Scatterplot for each dataset

q3

Based on the four scatterplots you created, what would you determine your next step in the analysis to be:
SOLUTION: Look for other predictor variables as there may not be a relationship between Age and Income

explanation

Adjusting for Outliers Explanation

Sometimes we may have a data set where there’s no association between two variables, for example here, there’s no relationship between age and income that we can determine. But an outlier is present and actually causing the relationship.

We see here where age and income are fairly random with no association between how old a person is and how much income they have.
But the outlier creates the slope of the line by just being present… so without outlier 1 (row 10), we have a steep positive slope, but without outliers 2 (row 14) and 3 (row 15), we have a negative slope.

In other words without the outlier we wouldn’t really be able to draw a legitimate line at all, but the presence of the outlier is what creates the model effect.
In cases such as this, we should definitely remove the outlier and investigate other predictor variables.
